I.
A taxonomy

Unsupervised is three jobs, not one.

The deliverable picks the algorithm — not the other way around. Start by asking: what does the stakeholder actually receive? A scatter plot for a slide deck points to PCA. A spreadsheet with a "segment" column points to K-Means. A ranked watch list of suspicious customers points to Isolation Forest. The mistake is choosing an algorithm first and hoping it produces something useful — that is how you spend a week on results nobody asked for.

II.
PCA

Scale first, or PC1 just tracks pounds.

PCA maximises variance. Unscaled, a feature in pounds (range 0–500) drowns out a feature in proportions (range 0–1). Same data, two PCA runs — on the right the principal component finds the joint pattern, not the loudest column.

✕ Unscaled

PC1 = spend in pounds.

The first principal component points almost exactly along the spend axis — because spend has the largest raw variance. PC1 carries 96% of the variance, but it's just one feature in disguise.

✓ StandardScaler

PC1 reads the joint pattern.

After Z-scoring, both features contribute on equal footing. PC1 catches what they have in common (e.g. an "engagement" direction); the explained variance is more honestly distributed.

PC1 var: 62%
spend loading: 0.71

How a principal component is built

A principal component (PC) is a new axis — a weighted linear combination of all your original features. PC1 is the direction in which the data spreads out the most; PC2 is the next most-spread direction that is uncorrelated with PC1, and so on.

PCA maximises variance because variance is a proxy for information. A direction where all points land on top of each other tells you nothing; a direction where they spread far apart reveals the most contrast between customers. By ranking axes from highest to lowest variance, PCA lets you keep the first few PCs and discard the rest without losing much signal.

Concretely: the loading (weight) on each original feature tells you how much that feature contributes to a PC. A PC with high loadings on tenure and avg_monthly_spend is an "engagement" axis. A PC with a dominant loading on a single raw feature is a sign that feature dominated by its scale — which is exactly what the unscaled example above shows.

III.
K-Means

Pick K. The plot rarely tells you.

Inertia drops smoothly. Silhouette rarely peaks sharply. The slider scans K=2 to 10 — watch the curves; watch the cluster sizes. A tiny 12-row cluster next to three 2,500-row clusters is almost always an anomaly cluster, not a segment.

Choose K

Inertia (teal) is the total squared distance from every point to its cluster centre — a measure of compactness. It always falls as K rises, so look for the elbow where the drop flattens out rather than the raw minimum. Silhouette (vermillion) compares how close each point is to its own cluster versus the nearest other cluster; it ranges from −1 to +1 and higher is better. Neither metric tells you the "true" K — they are evidence, not a verdict. The vertical line marks your chosen K.

VI.
A checklist

Three checks before you reach for an algorithm.

Skip these and you'll ship a result that is mathematically defensible but operationally useless — or one the stakeholder cannot read.

1

What is the deliverable?

A picture for a stakeholder is PCA. A segment per row is K-Means (or DBSCAN). A ranked list of weird rows is Isolation Forest. Pick the deliverable first — do not start the other way around.

✕ "Let's try K-Means and see what happens." ✓ "Marketing needs four named segments. K-Means with K=4."
2

Is preprocessing identical?

Median-impute, standard-scale, one-hot encode — exactly as in L03. No held-out test set means no second chance to catch a leakage bug. Tighten the discipline, don't loosen it.

✕ "It's unsupervised — do we need to scale?" ✓ "Same ColumnTransformer as the churn model."
3

Metric and sentence?

"K=4 because silhouette peaks there" is half an answer. The full one names the operational reason: campaigns marketing can run, segments the team can describe.

✕ "Silhouette peaked at K=4." ✓ "Silhouette + elbow support K=3-4, and marketing can run four campaigns but not seven."

VII.
Check yourself

Nine questions from the field notebook.

Try each mentally first. Click to flip the card. Numbering matches lesson.md.

▸ Click to reveal PCA on raw NorthStar data — PC1 is almost entirely avg_monthly_spend. What happened?
PCA maximises variance; spend (in pounds) has a much bigger raw range than ratios (0–1) or counts (0–10). Without scaling, its variance swamps everything. Fix: StandardScaler before PCA. Each feature then contributes equally; PC1 reflects the strongest joint pattern, not the loudest column.
▸ Click to reveal The first 2 PCs explain 22%; you need 6-8 to reach 80%. What does that tell you?
Variance is spread across many directions — the features are largely independent. PCA cannot compress aggressively here. A 2D plot is still useful for Marcus, but be explicit: it shows ~22% of the signal. The clustering downstream uses all 17 dimensions, not just the two on the screen.
▸ Click to reveal PC1 loads strongly on tenure, total_purchases, avg_monthly_spend. What is PC1?
An engagement / activity axis. Customers high on PC1 have stayed longer, bought more, spent more. To a stakeholder: "PC1 ranks customers from least to most engaged." The algorithm gives coefficients; you read them and supply the human label.
▸ Click to reveal Elbow plot has no clear bend; silhouette peaks weakly at K=2. What do you ship?
Real data rarely has sharp natural clusters under K-Means' spherical assumption. Two honest options: (a) pick K from business need ("four because marketing can run four campaigns") and be explicit that actionability decided it, or (b) try a different family (DBSCAN, hierarchical). Do not pretend the elbow showed something it did not.
▸ Click to reveal K=4 returns three clusters of ~2,500 and one of 12. Interpretation?
The 12-row cluster is an anomaly group K-Means could not assign elsewhere. Two options: report those 12 as a watch list alongside three real segments, or drop them as outliers and refit at K=3. Do not present "four equally important segments" — that misrepresents the result.
▸ Click to reveal How do you turn integer cluster labels into something Marcus can act on?
Group the data by cluster, take each feature's mean per cluster, compare to the global mean (Z-score for a heatmap). Each cluster has a profile — high on some features, low on others. Write one sentence per cluster: "Cluster 0: long tenure, high spend, low returns — Loyal Premium." Names + profile table, not integers.
▸ Click to reveal Fit Isolation Forest with contamination=0.05, then want 2%. Do you refit?
No. contamination only sets the threshold between +1 / -1 labels; the continuous score_samples() is independent of it. Keep the model, take the scores, pick a stricter percentile cutoff. That is why contamination is an operational dial, not a model parameter.
▸ Click to reveal Top anomaly: tenure 70mo, 0 purchases this quarter, 7 support tickets. Real? Action?
Yes — a long-tenured customer who suddenly stopped engaging and started complaining is genuinely unusual. No single Z-score would catch the combination. Action is not "label as fraud" — it is "send to customer success for a personal call." Report the diverging features alongside the flag.
▸ Click to reveal Anomalies are spread across all four clusters, not concentrated in one. What does that confirm?
Anomaly detection and clustering answer different questions: clusters are dense regions, anomalies are sparse edges — and edges exist around every cluster. If anomalies were concentrated in one cluster, that cluster itself would be an outlier group and you'd re-examine K. Spread anomalies confirm the two methods are complementary.
